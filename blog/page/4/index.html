Tierra del Fuego

By Jan | Published: January 31, 2008
One of the major attractions near Ushuaia is Tierra del Fuego National Park
Founded in 1960, this park is located on the Argentina-Chile border, at the southern end of the Andes mountain range. 
I can’t do the park justice, in words or in photographs. It’s a beautiful place, even during the chilly, drizzly time we were there.

We took a hike. It’s a beautiful place. We had been seeing such dry, dusty, flat, and harsh landscapes, that we were both thrilled by all the green around us and the trees. 


After deciding which photos to share today, I couldn’t help but notice something I didn’t realize before. Look at the color in the two photographs above …….. OK? Now look at the color of the yarn I purchased in town later in the day. Yeah, believe it or not, I didn’t notice at the time that this is exactly the color I had been drawn to on the hike. 
I found this wool yarn at a shop in town that carried lots of different hand crafted items. The girl working in the store wasn’t able to answer my detailed questions about the breed of sheep, or what type of dye was used to make this yarn. She did say it came from sheep raised in the region, so I’d like to think that it was dyed with madder root or the South American cochineal bug, since these are the natural sources for red dye. No facts to base that on, but no one to dispute it either! 
Thanks so much for letting me share this amazing trip with you. I’ve had a really good time and appreciate the comments and e-mails you’ve sent me. I love hearing from you guys!

Ushuaia, Argentina

By Jan | Published: January 30, 2008
Ushuaia, [oo-SWY-yah] for the first half of the 20th century was a penal colony, with activities centered around a large prison.  The building that served as the prison until 1947 now houses the Prison Museum, a Maritime Museum, an Art Museum, Museo del Fin del Mundo, and of course the requisite gift shops.  While the history of the prison and the maritime activities was interesting to read about, you want to know what really caught my eye? Don’t be shocked.  My eyes were attracted to the doors. Layers upon layers – of paint, of history, of bleak conditions, of grime and cold.  I wasn’t so much attracted to the thought of grime and cold and bleak conditions, but the layers of paint? Oh my.

Even knowing the history of the building, I couldn’t help but be captivated by the wooden doors.

Then, in a display case, these wood and bone clothespins. I love them and the care someone took to make them and to make a case for them. What I want to know is why anyone needs this type of holder/case for clothespins. My mother had a fabric bag that held clothespins on the line so she could easily reach them when hanging out the laundry. It might have even had some rick rack embellishment on it.  But this kind of case? What made it practical in the prison setting? Any ideas?
Chicago had Cows, Santa Fe had Ponies, Charlotte had Rocking Chairs, Victoria had Orcas, Ushuaia has Penguins.
When I was googling around for these links, I came across this wonderful entry. Take a look.
Only one more day on the trip, and then the blog will be back to it’s regularly scheduled programing. I’ll be sad to see the trip end and glad I took lots of photos along the way.

On the Road (& On the Water) Again……..

By Jan | Published: January 29, 2008
We hit the road again today. Since I’m not even close to being fluent in Spanish, and since I’m not completely convinced that anyone really knows how this bus system works, I’m a little anxious about this part of the trip. We have lots of connections to make today (no straight answer on exactly how many, which isn’t helping in the anxiety department), a border to cross (Chile back into Argentina), some water to get across (on a bus no less!), and a hostel waiting for us (I hope) at the other end. 

So far, so good. The ferry was large enough for the bus, plus a couple of cars. I had this vision, more like a nightmare actually, that the Straits of Magellan would be rough. Rough water, rough crossing, a long time on a boat. That’s what I was prepared for. What I got was this calm water and not even enough time to enjoy my mediocre cup of cafe con leche. Kind of anti-climatic really. Oh well, I guess it’s better to have a safe crossing than to have an exciting adventure story.

Now I see why lamb is on every menu here. I also understand why fleece needs to be washed before processing it. 

What a harsh landscape, don’t you think? Imagine when it gets really windy here – which is pretty much year round. 
Upside?    We’re sitting on upholstered bus seats today, not this well-traveled motorcycle. I guess when you plan this kind of trip, you have to carry your own spares. Not exactly a tire kingdom at every crossing. Are there even enough roads out here to have crossings? A few I guess.
Fourteen hours later, 4 successful bus and van connections, a few uneventful pit stops behind us, and we arrive in Ushuaia, the southernmost city in Argentina. This is where the cruise ships depart to Antarctica. We’re not doing that, but still, it’s a pretty cool town near the base of the mountains.

Our plan here, besides visiting the place that claims to be the southernmost city in the world? Penguins! Can’t see penguins without getting on a boat, so here we go through the Beagle Channel. Pretty lighthouse, isn’t it? I love lighthouses.

Today’s mission accomplished. Penguins. Magellan Penguins to be precise. It’s really windy and lots of them are lying down – I would guess that’d be a good way to combat the wind if you’re a penguin. The ones lying down look kinda like road kill in my photo, but since this is a tiny island, with no roads, trust me, they are just lying down. They’ve got a beautiful view of the Martial mountain range don’t they?

It was very cold on the boat ride. We finally had a need for all the layers of clothes we’d been carrying all over Argentina. It was so windy in fact, that the captain wouldn’t take us back to Ushuaia on the boat. Something about the wind, the waves, and it being a catamaran that it wouldn’t be safe. Hmmmm, we’re now on an island with the penguins and the captain won’t take us back. 
He called a bus to come pick us up.  Did I mention we’re on an island?
Yeah, that’s what I thought too. A couple of really cold hours (or maybe it just seemed like hours) later he took us a short distance across the channel to a ranch, where the buses were waiting, with their heaters running, to ‘rescue’ us all from the Beagle Channel and the weather. I’m really glad we booked the morning tour
, since I’m pretty sure the afternoon one didn’t even leave the harbor!
Next day, you would never have believed how windy and cold it had been. Look how calm the water is now.

Chile!

By Jan | Published: January 28, 2008
Next stop: Chile. 
We took a bus from El Calafate to Puerto Natales, Chile. Crossing the border took some time, since we had to unload everything from the bus to go through customs. From our experience, the Chilean border guards were a lot stricter on the rules than the Argentinean guards. Walking from the bus stop to our hostel, I had to pull out my camera to remember this incredible wooden gate.

When we arrived at our hostel, we found this loom in the common room, with that gorgeous basket of wool next to it! There were finished weavings in about every room of the hostel. I was hopeful that it was there for hostel guests to work on, but I was told it was the owner’s project……
This lovely metal building was the view from our window.
After a good nights sleep, we boarded yet another bus for our trip to Torres del Paine National Park (which is the reason we came to this town).  Here’s our first view of the Torres (towers) through the window of the bus.
We went for a long hike in the park. There was an incredible volume of water flowing here, and the color was (and is) absolutely amazing!
I was prepared for cool to cold weather. I had a backpack full of layers to don. After all, we were in Chile, right? It was incredibly clear and bright and the sun was very intense. The silk scarf I had brought for warmth was instead used to protect the top of my head from the sun!

How can anyone not love all these shades of green? Aren’t they incredible?
There were a couple of trees along the path, but not many. I’m really thankful someone invented sunscreen!
In addition to all the layers of clothing in my backpack, I also had a crochet project. We had 4-5 hours of bus time after all! We took a break in the shade on our hike back.
It was so dry and, well, it was really dry, that my shoes were no longer black at the end of the hike.
Torres del Paine was one of the most beautiful places I think I’ve ever seen.  If you go, I would advise that you be prepared for any weather imaginable. I’ve been told that one can experience 4 seasons in the span of 1 day there. We were incredibly fortunate to be there on such a spectacular day.  Next: back across the Argentina border and we’re heading farther south.

Perito Moreno Glacier

By Jan | Published: January 27, 2008
After Bariloche we flew to the small town of El Calafate, Argentina. It’s main tourist attraction is it’s access to, among other natural wonders,  the Perito Moreno Glacier, which is why this stop was on our itinerary. Here’s our first glimpse of the glacier, taken through the bus window. See the ice dam across the lake? That’s the glacier. Yeah, we came to trek on it. Still sounds like an interesting idea, yes?

After walking around and getting a variety of good views of the glacier, we boarded a boat to cross over the lake where we are to access the glacier.
I’m thinking, how the heck are we supposed to be able to walk on that????? Whose idea was this anyway! 
For a sense of scale, that’s my daughter standing on the rocks. I’m still wondering how we’re going to walk on that huge field of ice.
Here’s the racks that the crampons hang from when they are not being used. Crampons were required to trek on the glacier. Gladly, I thought, anything to help me walk on ice safely.
Even with crampons on, these fissures were something I didn’t like getting too close to……

My brain said “Don’t look down now”.  But my camera said, “Hey look, your foot is really close to the edge right now, how exciting”. The camera won.
Over peaks, through valleys, we slowly, and for the most part, safely, trekked around, over, across, and back again around the glacier. It’s amazing what we can do when we have no choice……. Seems the guides had done this trip before and knew that if we’d made it this far, we could use a drink. Whiskey and some freshly chopped glacial ice?  Yes please, make mine a double.
After removing our crampons and heading back to where the boat was to pick us up, I looked back and saw this amazing balancing act. I have no idea how I missed it on the way out…… perhaps I’m more in tune with nature after trekking on a glacier? Nah, someone must have just put that rock there when I wasn’t looking. 
On the boat ride back across the lake, we had to maneuver around some fallen bits (some pretty big bits) of glacial ice. We were supposed to be able to cruise really close to the glacier at this point, but the unseasonably warm weather they’d been having meant the glacier was cracking a lot (which sounds like loud gunshots or cannon fire)  and dropping more than it’s share of ice into the lake. Seems this can cause 6 foot waves on the lake. Our captain didn’t take us close to the glacier. Good man, that captain.

Museum of Patagonia

By Jan | Published: January 26, 2008

In downtown San Carlos de Bariloche we found the Museum of Patagonia:  a regional museum containing information associated with the Patagonian environment, the aboriginal population, the history of the territories and their conquest.


No matter where I am, I’m always attracted to grids. The patchwork grids decorating the backside of these animal hides are no exception. What an amazing example of the power and importance of decorative art.  I apologize for the glare in the following photo, but I wanted to share this amazing piece:

Those of you who have known me since my days of owning Batiks Etcetera already know how much I love dye processes and ikat. Those of you who didn’t know that before, are just learning how much I love dyes, and ikat fabrics…….

And I absolutely couldn’t resist my attraction to this leather pouch:
 


Just look at these wood and stone drop spindles for spinning yarn:

Thanks to my daughter asking several people, we found a small shop in Bariloche were I was able to choose some handspun yarn. This yarn was spun by a Mapuche woman. There were readymade sweaters, hats, gloves, etc. for sale in addition to a decent selection of natural color wools. Don’t bother to shop for yarn here if you don’t like natural colors, but if you love earth tones as much as I do, and you can name at least 5 different shades of brown, then it’s a great stop! I love buying from artists coops.

San Carlos de Bariloche

By Jan | Published: January 25, 2008
Buses are a major form of transportation in parts in Argentina. We booked our bus ticket from Neuquen to San Carlos de Bariloche and settled in for a comfortable ride. It was a great opportunity to enjoy the lunch we were served, drink some coffee, listen to my iPod, crochet, and watch the scenery change. Believe me, it was much better to pay attention to my crochet and the scenery than to watch how close the bus came to other vehicles on the road!
The Bariloche area is part of what’s called the Lake District of Patagonia, at the foothills of the Andes mountain range. Remembering how dry is was around Neuquen, we enjoyed seeing some green trees from the bus.

Once in Bariloche, we booked a couple of excursions. Nothing like some beautiful scenery to enjoy from the back of a horse. I’m really glad we remembered our hats and some serious sunscreen.

Waiting for us after horseback riding, was a wonderful lunch. Sausages, beef, lamb, salad, bread, and of course, local red wine.
With the daylight savings time that was in effect, it didn’t get dark in Patagonia until about 11 p.m. We had a gorgeous evening kayaking on Gutierrez Lake
Bariloche is a beautiful area with a year round variety of outdoor activities. I’d definitely enjoy getting back here someday. Oh, and I haven’t even talked about what a huge variety of chocolate is available in Bariloche. Oh, well, you’ll just have to imagine chocolate shops vying for customers. Let’s just say, we did out share of taste testing………

Dinosaurs!

By Jan | Published: January 24, 2008
My daughter did some research while we were planning our trip to Argentina and she read about several paleontological sites. Apparently, Patagonia is one of the richest places for fossils in the world and it still harbors a wealth of treasures. According to the info we received, the province of Neuquen in particular has become a “paradise” for paleontologists. We decided to make the effort to see one of the sites that was touted: Centro Paleontologico Lago Barreales.

We flew from Buenos Aires to Neuquen. While I was waiting for luggage, my daughter found the tourist information counter and got some great information on who to contact to get to the site: it’s not on the beaten path, by any stretch of the imagination. See that white building on the far side of the lake, in the distance after about a 2 hour drive outside of town? That’s where we’re headed. 
My daughter thought it would be fun to rent a car and try and find it on our own. I opted to pay for a car that came with a driver who was also multilingual. Smart move on my part, don’t you agree? Here’s what the site looked like when we pulled up.


It was first thing in the morning and no technicians were out working yet.
Finally, one tech started his day. Shoveling, then scraping the top layer, then sweeping the finer layers, then down on hands and knees to see what he might find today…….
Inside the “museum” we could see how they encase the bones they find to safely move them inside for further study.


It was a warm, clear, calm day and I’m glad we visited first thing in the morning…….
This is a the road as we headed back into town to catch our bus to the next stop on our Patagonian adventure. Makes you wonder how someone thought to start digging here………. in this exact spot…………. I’m so glad we had our driver/guide to take us back to town.
